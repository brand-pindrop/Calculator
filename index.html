No fixed price tag

These exposures are harder to quantify but no less real. A fraudulent insider's access to these resources creates liability that extends beyond any specific regulatory framework.

  • Stolen IP and source code — Proprietary algorithms, customer data, trade secrets, and source code become leverage for extortion or sold to competitors.
  • Extortion of former employers — A fraudulent insider with access to sensitive systems can demand payment to avoid releasing or selling stolen data.
  • SEC 8-K disclosure within 4 business days — Public companies must disclose material cybersecurity incidents, triggering stock volatility and investor scrutiny.
  • State breach-notification obligations — 50+ states have data-breach notification laws. A fraudulent insider incident triggers state-level notifications, potential fines, and class-action exposure.
  • Higher cyber-insurance premiums — A breach incident will increase insurance costs, add exclusions, or result in coverage denial on renewal.
  • Reputational loss — Customer trust, brand damage, and loss of competitive advantage extend far beyond the immediate incident.
